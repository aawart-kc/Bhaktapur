Bhaktapur (Bhadgaon) – Historical Overview

Location

Bhaktapur is a city in the Kathmandu Valley, about 13 km east of Kathmandu. It is known as the “City of Devotees” or “City of Culture” for its well-preserved medieval architecture and rich Newari heritage.

Founding and Early History

Founded in the 12th century by King Ananda Dev Malla (though some sources trace roots to the 9th century), Bhaktapur became a major trade and cultural center in the Kathmandu Valley.

It was one of the three main city-states in the valley, along with Kathmandu (Kantipur) and Lalitpur (Patan).

Medieval Era

Bhaktapur flourished under the Malla dynasty (12th–18th century). Known for its palaces, temples, and squares:

  • Durbar Square – royal palace complex with courtyards, statues, and temples.
  • Nyatapola Temple – a 5-story pagoda, tallest in Nepal.

It was a hub for artisans, metalworkers, and woodcarvers, shaping the unique Newari art and architecture.

Trade and Culture

Bhaktapur was famous for pottery, weaving, and handicrafts. It served as a center for festivals and rituals, preserving Newar traditions. The city maintained its own governance and culture, even as Kathmandu and Patan competed politically.

Modern Era

In 1769, Bhaktapur was annexed by Prithvi Narayan Shah, the founder of modern Nepal, ending Malla rule. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, attracting tourists for its historic squares, temples, and traditional Newari cuisine.

Cultural Highlights

  • Festivals: Bisket Jatra, Gai Jatra, Indra Jatra, Yomari Punhi
  • Cuisine: Bara, Chatamari, Yomari, Kwati, Aila
  • Architecture: Brick buildings, intricate wood carvings, stone sculptures, and pagoda-style temples

Extended History

Bhaktapur, historically known as “Bhadgaon”, literally means “City of Devotees”. Archaeological evidence suggests the area was inhabited over a thousand years ago, even before the Malla period. The city’s location in the Kathmandu Valley made it strategically important for trade, agriculture, and defense.

Malla Dynasty Era (12th–18th Century)

The Malla kings greatly influenced Bhaktapur’s growth and architecture. The city was divided into wards and neighborhoods, each with its own temples and squares.

  • King Ananda Dev Malla: credited with founding the city in its medieval form.
  • King Bhupatindra Malla (1696–1722): oversaw the construction of Nyatapola Temple and the 55-Window Palace, marking the city’s cultural peak.

Bhaktapur became famous for arts, festivals, and Newari craftsmanship including pottery, wood carving, and metalwork.

Architecture and Urban Design

Bhaktapur is famous for Durbar Square, Nyatapola Temple, 55-Window Palace, narrow streets, and water management through stone fountains (dhunge dharas) and ponds.

Trade, Festivals, and Annexation

Bhaktapur was a prosperous trade hub and preserved Newar rituals. In 1769, it was annexed by Prithvi Narayan Shah, uniting it into the modern Kingdom of Nepal. Many royal structures were damaged, but the city retained its cultural and architectural identity.

Modern Legacy

Bhaktapur is now a tourist hotspot, famous for cuisine, crafts, and heritage. It showcases Newar language, art, architecture, festivals, and culinary traditions. Despite modernization, it retains a unique identity, blending history, religion, and artistry.
