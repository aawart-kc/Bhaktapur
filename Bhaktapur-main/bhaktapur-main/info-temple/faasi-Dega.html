Fassi-Dega Temple

Echoes of Faith and Stone

Fassi-Dega, nestled in the heart of Bhaktapur Durbar Square, is a serene and sacred Shiva temple that has witnessed centuries of devotion, festivals, and resilience. Its dome-shaped architecture sets it apart from the traditional pagoda temples in the area.

Often called Silu Mahadev, this temple becomes the spiritual gateway for pilgrims during the Silu Jatra — a long walking journey to Gosaikunda that begins right here in Bhaktapur.

1. Origins & Era: Built in the 17th century during the Malla period, the temple reflects a unique mix of Indian shikhara and Newari design.

2. Earthquake Resilience: After the 2015 earthquake, the temple was damaged but restored using traditional materials to preserve its heritage.

3. Silu Jatra Pilgrimage: Each year, devotees gather here and begin a multi-day foot journey to Gosaikunda Lake, chanting and playing traditional instruments.

4. Spiritual Significance: Lord Shiva, worshipped as the destroyer of evil, is believed to protect Bhaktapur from natural disasters and hardship.

5. Hidden Secret: Locals say that when you ring the bell inside the temple with a true heart, your wish travels up to Gosaikunda — across the hills.

Fassi-Dega stands as a timeless symbol of Bhaktapur’s rich spiritual heritage. This unique temple, with its dome-shaped design, offers a peaceful sanctuary amidst the bustling Durbar Square. It has been a focal point for worship and community gatherings for centuries. The temple’s architecture blends traditional craftsmanship with influences that make it distinct in the cityscape. Pilgrims and visitors alike find solace here, drawn by the temple’s serene atmosphere and its role in local traditions. Throughout the year, Fassi-Dega remains a place where faith, culture, and history converge — quietly preserving the spirit of Bhaktapur for generations to come.

Where is Fassi-Dega?

Fassi-Dega lies right inside Bhaktapur Durbar Square, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Surrounded by other historical gems, it’s a peaceful spot often overlooked by fast-moving tourists.
