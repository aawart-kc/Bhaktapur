Tihar

Tihar: The Festival of Lights, Life, and Harmony

Tihar is one of Nepal’s most cherished festivals — a vibrant five-day celebration of light, love, animals, and the divine bond between brothers and sisters. Known as Deepawali in other parts of South Asia, it brings homes and hearts to life with flickering oil lamps, colorful rangolis, and songs of blessing.

1. Origin & Mythology

-Tihar is deeply rooted in Hindu mythology, especially the stories surrounding Yama, the god of death, and his sister Yamuna.

-According to legend, Yamuna asked Yama to postpone her brother’s death until she could spend time with him. Touched by her love and rituals, Yama granted him long life. This bond is celebrated on Bhai Tika, the fifth day of Tihar.

-Other myths honor the goddess Laxmi, who visits clean and beautifully lit homes to bless families with wealth and prosperity.


2. Historical Background

-Tihar has been celebrated in Nepal for centuries and is closely tied with agrarian traditions, animal worship, and family unity. It blends Vedic rituals with local Newar customs, making it unique to Nepal.

-In the Newar community, the festival is known as "Swanti" and includes extra rituals like Mha Puja (self-worship) on the fourth day, which honors the body and soul.


3. When Is It Celebrated?

-Tihar is celebrated in October or November, depending on the lunar calendar (Kartik month).

-It begins two days after Dashain ends and lasts for five days, each day dedicated to a specific theme and deity.


4. Key Days and Rituals

Kaag Tihar (Day 1)

Crows are worshipped as messengers of death. People offer rice and sweets on rooftops to appease them and avoid misfortune.

Kukur Tihar (Day 2)

Dogs are honored for their loyalty and connection to Yama. They are adorned with garlands, tika, and offered delicious food.

Gai Tihar and Laxmi Puja (Day 3)

In the morning, cows — symbols of prosperity — are worshipped. At night, homes are decorated with lights and rangoli to welcome Goddess Laxmi. People perform Deusi-Bhailo, a traditional singing and dancing custom.

Govardhan Puja / Mha Puja (Day 4)

Oxen are worshipped, and in Newar households, Mha Puja is performed to purify the soul and bless the self for the New Year.

Bhai Tika (Day 5)

Sisters put multi-colored tika on their brothers' foreheads, offer flower garlands and sweets, and pray for their longevity. In return, brothers give gifts and promise protection.


5. Cultural Significance

-Festival of light and life, symbolizing the victory of good over evil, life over death, and harmony among humans, animals, and gods.

-Strengthens family bonds, especially between siblings on Bhai Tika.

-Promotes animal respect and gratitude toward nature’s helpers like dogs, cows, and crows.


6. Tourist Tip

If you’re visiting:

-Evenings during Laxmi Puja are magical — walk around Bhaktapur or Patan to see dazzling oil lamps and vibrant rangoli art.

-Join a Deusi-Bhailo group to experience local songs, dance, and hospitality.

-Respect rituals and ask before photographing animals or home altars.


7. Hidden Gems

-Rangoli art is traditionally handmade using colored powders, flowers, and rice flour — often shaped like lotus flowers to invite Laxmi.

-In some communities, Yama Deep (a small oil lamp) is lit and placed at the doorway throughout the festival to honor the god of death and protect the household.

-Tihar sweets include sel roti, anarse, and fini, prepared fresh and shared with neighbors.
